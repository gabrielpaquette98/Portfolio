Surfpunk

Surfpunk is a PvE co-op extraction ARPG by Double Stallion Games, where players explore island-filled worlds and gather resources as daring raiders. When I joined the project, the team was transitioning to 3D, and my primary mission was to translate the studio's distinctive 2D style into 3D while preserving the essence of a "Double Stallion game." Early in development, the game pivoted from a 2D platformer to a top-down perspective, giving me the opportunity to contribute to a variety of systems over nearly two years. My work included cel-shading for characters and environments, water effects, outlines, artist tools, and more!

Cel Shading and Decals

My first task was creating a shader to replicate Double Stallion's signature aesthetic. This shader utilizes a gradient texture to adjust how lighting affects shading, allowing seamless transitions from smooth to cel-shaded effects. It supports multiple features such as specular highlights, rim lights, emissive textures, and outline options. Depending on whether the object is a character or an environmental element, additional techniques ‚Äîlike triplanar texturing, silhouette shading, vertex displacement for ink effects on enemies, and wind effects‚Äî are applied.

Later, I developed a decal shader variant of the main cel shader. Using the depth buffer, it recalculates where the position in world space is, which allows for decals. The same code is reused for some visual effects that use different shaders made specifically for those effects. They are animated by sending the current time and the starting time of the effect to the effect's shader.

Water, Foam and Waves

Water was a significant focus during my time on Surfpunk. I implemented many things, such as caustics, refraction, and depth-based recoloring of submerged objects. I was allowed a lot of liberty on how it looks!

For the low-amplitude waves (seen in the first video), a big focus was put on making them readable and predictable. All the connected players needed to have the same waves, and these waves needed to be visually impactful while minimally affecting player movement.

Tackling intersection foam presented an interesting challenge. A popular way to do this is to use the depth buffer: recolor the objects that are under the water, and make them into foam if they are close to the surface. A problem with this approach is that it can only place foam on top of objects. If you were to look at a building from the side, the foam wouldn't extrude as expected. To fix this, I came up with a few solutions, most important of which was to bake an intersection texture to identify where the objects intersect with the water, and add to that texture the distance from the intersection. The result is exaggerated on the gif that shows the paddles of the ship, I got to generate those automatically from the model given by our environment artist.

For the tsunami wave appearing at the level's conclusion, I created a designer-friendly system based on a modifiable curve. This curve drives gameplay mechanics and vertex displacement of a plane, augmented by a secondary foam model. The wave's shading adheres to the water's overall principles.

Procedural Generation

When given the chance to select my own tasks for a sprint, I prototyped a system for procedural island generation. Designed as an editor tool, this system generates island terrain by generating first a grid of triangles and squares to deform tiles, producing varied islands from minimal input. Inspired by Oskar St√•lberg's work, this approach offers a starting point for designers to iterate and expand on, instead of his focus on producing a complete result at runtime.

Outlines and Selection Outline

Outlines, another early challenge, required a blend of techniques to achieve consistent results. The primary method involved rendering object IDs to a buffer for post-processing. Differences in this buffer generated outline pixels, with optional depth and normal-based enhancements in specific scenarios. This ID-based technique gave the most reliable results and became the foundation for the project's outlines.

Texture Painting and Texture Blending

To streamline texture painting on environment objects, I developed a tool allowing artists to paint directly in-engine, enabling real-time feedback with level props like grass and trees that would be tedious to set up in the artist's modeling software. The tool detects the cursor's location on objects and applies RGBA channel-based texture painting, and the resulting texture is sent to the shader where the textures are blended accordingly.

Inverse Kinematics and Legs Management

For animating the enemies walk cycle, I used Unity's Animation Rigging Package to create a procedural system for enemy foot placement. This solution calculates foot positions dynamically, allowing enemies to decide when to step. It integrates with the packages' IK solver to ensure smooth animations while reducing animator workload. In this example, the animator only needed to animate parts of the model and let the root and the feet still. The feet and the body are then moved by my code, and it uses the distance from the animation poses to determine when to take steps.
